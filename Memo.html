The corridor position is the decision to win

ToMost Rev. William Wack · Bishop
David Kimbell · CFO
FromJarah Jacquay · Managing Principal
ReBeulah Road — recommended next steps
DateMay 7, 2026

One decision on the Beulah Road parcel outweighs the rest: whether the Diocese shapes the corridor that will run at its edge, or inherits the one others draw. This memo states the case and the next two moves.

The FDOT corridor study and the county's OLF-8 master plan are both in their formative window — road alignment, density, and access for the next two years are being settled now, largely by counterparties already at the table: Escambia County, FDOT, GMC, and Crabtree Group. The Diocese holds roughly 58 contiguous acres against that area with full optionality intact — but is a landowner among the parties, not yet one shaping the outcome. The street-typology commitment is the single largest lever on value, and each quarter the parcel sits undefined, that optionality quietly erodes.

Key Finding

The corridor commitment is decided by others unless the Diocese moves first — and it is worth $6–17M on this parcel alone.

Risk

Informal assurances on right-of-way do not survive a change in counterparty. The cost of waiting is paid in lost optionality, not in fees.

Recommendation

Lock a corridor position now; pursue a ground-lease or PPP structure that keeps mission control while value compounds.

Recommended next steps
1
Authorize a corridor position briefA one-page statement of the Diocese's preferred alignment and access, ready to file with FDOT and the county before the next study milestone.
~2 weeks
2
Commission the feasibility scopeEngage Crabtree Group on the civil work that converts a corridor position into an entitlement and disposition strategy.
30–45 days

Q3 2026

The land is leverage, not inventory. Moving first on the corridor is the lowest-cost, highest-value decision available to the Diocese this year.
